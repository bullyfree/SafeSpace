Learn About Bullying

Understanding what bullying is and how to prevent it is the first step to creating a safer world for everyone.

What is Bullying?

Bullying is unwanted, aggressive behavior that involves a real or perceived power imbalance. The behavior is repeated, or has the potential to be repeated, over time.

For something to be considered bullying, it must include:

  • An Imbalance of Power: Kids who bully use their power—such as physical strength, access to embarrassing information, or popularity—to control or harm others.
  • Repetition: Bullying behaviors happen more than once or have the potential to happen more than once.
  • Intent to Harm: The person bullying has a goal to cause harm. Bullying is not accidental.

Remember!

Not all conflict is bullying. Friends sometimes disagree and that's normal. Bullying involves a power imbalance and the intent to hurt someone repeatedly.

Types of Bullying

Bullying can take many forms. Here are the main types:

Physical Bullying

Includes hitting, kicking, pushing, taking or breaking someone's things, and making mean or rude hand gestures.

Verbal Bullying

Saying or writing mean things, such as teasing, name-calling, inappropriate sexual comments, or threatening to cause harm.

Social Bullying

Sometimes called relational bullying, involves hurting someone's reputation or relationships, such as leaving someone out on purpose, spreading rumors, or embarrassing someone in public.

Cyberbullying

Takes place over digital devices like cell phones, computers, and tablets through social media, text messages, chats, and websites.

Cyberbullying

Cyberbullying is bullying that takes place over digital devices like cell phones, computers, and tablets.

Did You Know?

Cyberbullying can happen 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, and can reach a kid even when they are alone. It can happen any time of the day or night.

Examples of Cyberbullying:

  • Sending hurtful text messages or emails
  • Posting mean or hurtful comments or images on social media
  • Creating fake profiles to embarrass someone
  • Excluding someone from online groups
  • Spreading rumors or lies online

Tips to Prevent Cyberbullying:

  • Think before you post or send anything online
  • Keep your passwords private
  • Don't respond to mean or hurtful messages
  • Save evidence of cyberbullying
  • Block people who are bullying you online
  • Report cyberbullying to a trusted adult

How to Stand Up Against Bullying

When you see bullying happen, you can make a difference by taking action. Here's how:

1

Be an Upstander, Not a Bystander

When you see bullying, don't just stand by and watch. Speaking up or getting help can make a big difference.

2

Support the Person Being Bullied

Show them that you care by talking to them privately, sitting with them at lunch, or walking with them between classes.

3

Tell a Trusted Adult

Report the bullying to a parent, teacher, school counselor, or other trusted adult who can help address the situation.

4

Speak Up (When Safe)

If it's safe to do so, tell the person bullying that their behavior is not okay. Often, hearing this from a peer can be powerful.

"In the end, we will remember not the words of our enemies, but the silence of our friends."

— Martin Luther King Jr.

The Anti-Bullying Pledge

I pledge to:

  • Treat everyone with kindness and respect
  • Stand up for others who are being bullied
  • Include those who are left out
  • Report bullying when I see it happening
  • Think before I post anything online
  • Be a friend to someone who needs one
  • Never participate in bullying behavior
  • Ask for help when I need it
